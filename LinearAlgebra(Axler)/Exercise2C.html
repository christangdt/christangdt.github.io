                                      1

                                      Suppose V is finite-dimensional and U is a subspace of V such that dim U=dim V. Prove that U=V.

                                      Proof: Assume U≠V, then there is v∈V,v∉U, obviously v≠0, extend v to a basis of V, namely v,v2,…,vdim V, since this list spans V, each vector u∈U can be expressed as a linear combination of this list. Thus the list v,v2,…,vdim V also spans U, by 2.42 and the condition dim U=dim V, we see this list is also a basis of U, which means v∈U, a contradiction.

                                      2

                                      Show that the subspaces of R2 are precisely {0},R2 and all lines in R2 through the origin.

                                      Solution: The subsets listed are obviously subspaces of R2. To see these are the only subspaces of R2, let U be a subspace of R2 and then dim U can only be 0,1 or 2. If dim U=0 then U={0}, if dim U=2 then R2, if dim U=1 then U has a basis with only one vector, let it be u=(x,y)≠0, then U={au,a∈R} which is a line through the origin, and any line through the origin can be expressed in this form, just choose the point on the line with x-coordinate being 1, the corresponding y-coordinate being y0, and let u=(1,y0).

                                      9

                                      Suppose v1,…,vm is linearly independent in V and w∈V. Prove that

                                      dim span(v1+w,…,vm+w)≥m−1.

                                      Proof: Let S=span(v1+w,…,vm+w), then v1−vm,…,vm−1−vm belong to S. If a1,…,am−1 are scalars such that

                                      a1(v1−vm)+⋯+am−1(vm−1−vm)=0

                                      or

                                      a1v1+⋯+am−1vm−1−(a1+⋯+am−1)vm=0

                                      this means a1,…,am−1 are all 0. Since the list v1−vm,…,vm−1−vm is linearly independent, we have dim⁡S≥m−1.

                                      10

                                      Suppose p0,p1,…,pm∈P(F) are such that each pj has degree j . Prove that p0,p1,…,pm is a basis of Pm(F).

                                      Proof: Since p0 has degree 0 and p1 has degree 1, we can express z as a linear combination of p0 and p1, similarly, each zi with i≤m can be expressed as a linear combination of p0,…,pm, thus it spans Pm(F). Now let

                                      a0p0+⋯+ampm=0

                                      since only pm has degree m, then the coefficient of zm is determined only by am, thus am=0, then the coefficient of zm−1 is uniquely determined by am−1, thus am−1=0, continue this process we see a0=⋯=am=0, thus p0,p1,…,pm is linearly independent, so it is a basis of Pm(F).

                                      11

                                      Suppose that U and W are subspaces of R8 such that dim U=3, dim W=5, and U+W=R8. Prove that R8=U⊕W.

                                      Proof: Assume v∈U∩W,v≠0, extend v to a basis of U, namely v,u2,u3, extend v to a basis of W, namely v,w2,w3,w4,w5. Then the list v,u2,u3,w2,w3,w4,w5 spans U+W, thus dim⁡R8≤7, which is a contradiction. So we must have U∩W=∅.

                                      12

                                      Suppose that U and W are both five-dimensional subspaces of R9. Prove that U∩W≠{0}.

                                      Proof: Assume U∩W={0}. Choose a basis u1,…,u5 of U and a basis w1,…,w5 of W, if scalars a1,…,a10 satisfy the equation

                                      a1u1+⋯+a5u5+a6w1+⋯+a10w5=0

                                      we see that if u=a1u1+⋯+a5u5 and w=a6w1+⋯+a10w5, then u=−w, which means u,w∈U∩W, thus u=w=0, this further means a1=⋯=a10=0, as basis of U and W are linearly independent. We conclude that u1,…,u5,w1,…,w5 are linearly independent in R9, which is a contradiction since dim R9=9.

                                      13

                                      Suppose that U and W are both four-dimensional subspaces of C6. Prove that there exist two vectors in U∩W such that neither of these vectors is a scalar multiple of the other.

                                      Proof: Any basis of U consists of 4 vectors, at least two of them belongs to W, if not, then we can from the space W−U add more than 2 vectors to the basis and still get a linearly independent set, the length of this list is more than 6, which is a contradiction. Now the two vectors in the basis of U are in W, and neither of the two vectors is a scalar multiple of the other, since they are linearly independent.

                                      14

                                      Suppose U1,…,Um are finite-dimensional subspaces of V. Prove that U1+⋯+Um is finite-dimensional and

                                      dim(U1+⋯+Um)≤dim U1+⋯+dim Um

                                      Proof: We only need to prove the case for m=2, and use induction to do the rest. Let dim U1=k and dim U2=n, the subspace U1∩U2 has dimension 0≤d≤min(k,n). Thus choose a basis v1,…,vd for U1∩U2, add k−d vectors to get a basis for U1, add n−d vectors to get a basis for U2, then the two list combined can span U1+U2, thus we have

                                      dim⁡(U1+U2)≤(d+k−d+n−d)≤k+n=dim⁡U1+dim⁡U2

                                      V=U1⊕⋯⊕Un

                                      Proof: Let v1,…,vn be a basis for V, and Ui=span (vi), since each vector v∈V can be uniquely represented as a linearly combination of v1,…,vn, we get the result.

                                      16

                                      Suppose U1,…,Um are finite-dimensional subspaces of V such that U1+⋯+Um is a direct sum. Prove that U1⊕⋯⊕Un is finite-dimensional and

                                      dim⁡(U1⊕⋯⊕Um)=dim⁡U1+⋯+dim⁡Um

                                      Proof: We only need to prove the case for m=2, and use induction to do the rest. Let dim U1=k and dim U2=n, since U1∩U2={0}, let v1,…,vk be a basis for U1 and u1,…,un be a basis for U2, then v1,…,vk,u1,…,un is a basis for the subspace U1⊕U2, thus dim⁡(U1⊕U2)=dim⁡U1+dim⁡U2.

                                      17

                                      Prove or give a counterexample: if U1,U2,U3 are subspaces of a finite-dimensional vector space, then

                                      dim (U1+U2+U3)=dim U1+dim U2+dim U3−dim (U1∩U2)−dim (U1∩U3)−dim (U2∩U3)+dim (U1∩U2∩U3)

                                      Solution: A counterexample is U1=(x,0),U2=(0,y),U3=(x,x) with x,y∈R. They are subspaces of R2, with dim⁡U1=dim⁡U2=dim⁡U3=1, since U1+U2+U3=R2, we have dim⁡(U1+U2+U3)=2. But we have

                                      U1∩U2=U1∩U3=U2∩U3={(0,0)}

                                      and it follows that U1∩U2∩U3={(0,0)}, so the right side of the equation is 3, while the left side is 2.
