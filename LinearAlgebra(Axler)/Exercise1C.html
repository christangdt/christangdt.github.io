                                                    1

                                                    For each of the following subsets of F3, determine whether it is a subspace of F3:

                                                    (a) {(x1,x2,x3)∈F3:x1+2x2+3x3=0};

                                                    (b) {(x1,x2,x3)∈F3:x1+2x2+3x3=4};

                                                    (c) {(x1,x2,x3)∈F3:x1x2x3=0};

                                                    (d) {(x1,x2,x3)∈F3:x1=5x3}.

                                                    Solution:

                                                    (a) is a subspace. (b) is not since (0,0,0) is not in it. (c) is not since (0,1,1) and (1,0,0) is in it but there sum is not. (d) is a subspace.

                                                    2

                                                    Verify all the assertions in Example 1.35.

                                                    Solution:

                                                    (a) If b=0, then it is obviously a subspace. Conversely, since (0,0,0,0) is in it, we have 0=5⋅0+b, which means b=0.

                                                    (b) The zero function f(x)=0 is in the set. Suppose f,g are in the set, then f+g and cf are continuous on [0,1].

                                                    (c) The zero function f(x)=0 is in the set. Suppose f,g are in the set, then f+g and cf are differentiable on [0,1].

                                                    (d) If b=0, then it is obviously a subspace. Conversely, let f,g be in the set, then f′(2)=b and g′(2)=b, while (f+g)′(2)=f′(2)+g′(2)=2b, thus 2b=b and b=0.

                                                    (e) The zero sequence {0,0,…} is in it, if {an} and {bn} are in the set, then {an+bn} and {can} are in it since liman+bn=0 and limcan=0.

                                                    3

                                                    Show that the set of differentiable real-valued functions f on the interval (−4,4) such that f′(−1)=3f(2) is a subspace of R(−4,4).

                                                    Proof: The zero function f(x)=0,x∈(−4,4) satisfies f′(−1)=3f(2). If f and g are in the set, then

                                                    (f+g)′(−1)=f′(−1)+g′(−1)=3[f(2)+g(2)]=3(f+g)(2)(cf)′(−1)=cf′(−1)=c3f(2)=3cf(2)=3(cf)(2),c∈R

                                                    which means f+g and cf are in the set.

                                                    9

                                                    A function f:R→R is called periodic if there exists a positive number p such that f(x)=f(x+p) for all x∈R. Is the set of periodic functions from R to R a subspace of RR? Explain.

                                                    Solution: Let f(x)=cos⁡x and g(x)=cos⁡πx, then both f and g are periodic. But f+g is not, assume so, then (f+g)(0)=(f+g)(p) for some positive p, this means 2=cos⁡p+cos⁡πp, thus cos⁡p=cos⁡πp=1, which is impossible.

                                                    10

                                                    Suppose U1 and U2 are subspaces of V. Prove that the intersection U1∩U2 is a subspace of V.

                                                    Proof: Let u∈U=U1∩U2, then u∈U1 and u∈U2. For additive identity, we have 0∈U1 and 0∈U2, thus 0∈U. To see U is closed under addition, we have

                                                    u,w∈U⟹(u,w∈U1)∧(u,w∈U2)⟹(u+w∈U1)∧(u+w∈U2)⟹u+w∈U

                                                    That U is closed under scalar multiplication can be similarly proved.

                                                    11

                                                    Prove that the intersection of every collection of subspaces of V is a subspace of V.

                                                    Proof: Let F={Uα} be a collection of subspaces of V. Since 0∈Uα for every Uα∈F, we have 0∈⋂αUα, next we have

                                                    u,w∈⋂αUα⟹u,w∈Uα,∀α⟹u+w∈Uα,∀α⟹u+w∈⋂αUα

                                                    and

                                                    a∈F,u∈⋂αUα⟹a∈F,u∈Uα,∀α⟹au∈Uα,∀α⟹au∈⋂αUα

                                                    12

                                                    Prove that the union of two subspaces of V is a subspace of V if and only if one of the subspaces is contained in the other.

                                                    Proof: One direction is obvious. Suppose U1 and U2 are two subspaces of V and U1∪U2 is still a subspace, we assume there exist u∈U1−U2 and w=U2−U1, since u,w∈U1∪U2, we have u+w∈U1∪U2 and thus either u+w∈U1 or u+w∈U2, if u+w∈U1, then w=u+w−u∈U1, a contradiction. If u+w∈U2, then u=u+w−w∈U2, a contradiction. Thus at least one of u,w is 0, which means one of the subspaces is contained in the other.

                                                    A function f:R→R is called even if f(−x)=f(x) for all x∈R, called odd if f(−x)=−f(x) for all x∈R. Let Ue denote the set of real-valued even functions on R and let Uo denote the set of real-valued odd functions on R. Show that RR=Ue⊕Uo.

                                                    Proof: First consider f(x)∈RR, if we let

                                                    g(x)=f(x)+f(−x)2,h(x)=f(x)−f(−x)2

                                                    then f(x)=g(x)+h(x), and we have

                                                    g(−x)=f(−x)+f(x)2=g(x),h(−x)=f(−x)−f(x)2=−h(x)

                                                    which means g(x)∈Ue and h(x)∈Uo, since f is arbitrary, we see RR=Ue+Uo. Next, if f(x)∈Ue∩Uo, then

                                                    f(−x)=−f(x)=f(x)⟹2f(x)=0⟹f(x)=0,∀x∈R

                                                    thus Ue∩Uo={0}, and RR=Ue⊕Uo.
