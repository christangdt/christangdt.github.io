                                Suppose b,c∈R. Define T:R3→R2 by

                                T(x,y,z)=(2x−4y+3z+b,6x+cxyz)

                                Show that T is linear if and only if b=c=0.

                                Proof: If b=c=0, then T is linear. Suppose T is linear, then T(0,0,0)=(0,0), so b=0, then T(1,1,1)=(1,6+c) and T(2,2,2)=(2,12+8c) by definition, since T(2,2,2)=2T(1,1,1), we have 12+8c=2(6+c), or 6c=0.

                                Proof: Let a1,…,am be scalars such that

                                a1v1+⋯+amvm=0

                                T(a1v1+⋯+amvm)=a1Tv1+⋯+amTvm=0

                                which means a1=⋯=am=0.

                                Solution: Define φ(c)=c― for all c∈C, then φ is not linear since

                                10

                                Suppose U is a subspace of V with U≠V. Suppose S∈L(U,W) and S≠0 (which means that Su≠0 for some u∈U). Define T:V→W by

                                Tv={Svif v∈U,0if v∈V and v∉U.

                                Prove that T is not a linear map on V.

                                Proof: Let u∈U and w∈V−U which satisfy Su≠0 and w≠0, then u+w∉U, otherwise w=u+w−u∈U, a contradiction to w∈V−U. Thus we have T(u+w)=0, but Tu=Su≠0 and Tw=0, so we have

                                T(u+w)≠Tu+Tw

                                which shows T is not a linear map.

                                11

                                Suppose V is finite-dimensional. Prove that every linear map on a subspace of V can be extended to a linear map on V. In other words, show that if U is a subspace of V and S∈L(U,W), then there exists T∈L(V,W) such that Tu=Su for all u∈U.

                                Proof: Let u1,…,um be a basis of U, then it can be extend to a basis of V, namely u1,…,um,vm+1,…,vn. Since S∈L(U,W), we have Sui=wi∈W for i=1,…,m, now define T∈L(V,W) as follows:

                                T(a1u1+⋯+amum+am+1vm+1+⋯+anvn)=a1w1+⋯+amwm

                                since Tui=Sui for i=1,…,m, we see that T and S are the same on a basis of U, which means T=S on U.

                                12

                                Suppose V is finite-dimensional with dim⁡V>0, and suppose W is infinite-dimensional. Prove that L(V,W) is infinite-dimensional.

                                Proof: That dim⁡V>0 means we can find v∈V such that v≠0. Extend v to a basis of V by add v2,…,vm vectors (possibly zero vectors added). Given any positive integer N, we can find a linear independent list w1,…,wN in W, now define Ti:V→W by

                                Ti(a1v+a2v2+⋯+amvm)=a1wi,i=1,…,N

                                It is easy to verify each Ti∈L(V,W), suppose there are scalars c1,…,cN such that

                                (c1T1+⋯+cNTN)v=0

                                then it means

                                c1w1+⋯+cNwN=0

                                thus c1=⋯=cN=0, so T1,…,TN are linearly independent, and dim⁡L(V,W)≥N, since this is true for any integer N, L(V,W) is infinite-dimensional.
