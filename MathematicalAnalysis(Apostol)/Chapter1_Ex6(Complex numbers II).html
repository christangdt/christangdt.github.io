                      Ex 1.35

                      Recall the definition of the inverse tangent: given a real number t, tan−1⁡(t) is the unique real number θ which satisfies the two conditions

                      −π2<θ<+π2,tan⁡θ=t.

                      If z=x+iy, show that a) arg⁡(z)=tan−1⁡(yx),if x>0 b) arg⁡(z)=tan−1⁡(yx)+π,if x<0,y≥0 c) arg⁡(z)=tan−1⁡(yx)−π,if x<0,y<0 d) arg⁡(z)=π2 if x=0,y>0;arg⁡(z)=−π2 if x=0,y<0.

                      Solution

                      By the definition of arg⁡(z) we know that tan⁡(arg⁡(z))=y/x, but since arg⁡(z)∈(−π,π] and tan−1⁡(y/x)∈(−π/2,π/2], we need to modify due to the conditions of x,y. a) if x>0, then arg⁡(z)∈(−π/2,π/2) since cos⁡arg⁡(z)>0 b) if x<0,y≥0, then cos⁡arg⁡(z)<0 and sin⁡arg⁡(z)≥0, this means arg⁡(z)∈(π/2,π) c) Similarly we get arg⁡(z)∈(−π/2,0) d) by direct calculation of arg⁡(z).

                      Ex 1.36

                      Define the following "pseudo-ordering" of the complex numbers: we say z1<z2 if we have either i)|z1|<|z2| or ii) |z1|=|z2| and arg⁡(z1)<arg⁡(z2). Which of Axioms 6,7,8,9 are satisfied by this relation?

                      Solution

                      Axiom 6 is satisfied. For any two complex numbers, both their norms and principal arguments are real, thus can be compared. Axiom 7 is not satisfied, consider x=1−i,y=1+i, since |x|=|y| and arg⁡(x)=−π/4<arg⁡(y)=π/4, we have x<y, but x−i=1−2i and y−i=1, we have |x−i|>|y−i| thus x−i>y−i. Axiom 8 is satisfied, since under this ordering we have z>0 for all z. Axiom 9 is satisfied, x>y and y>z means |x|≥|z|, if |x|>|z| then x>z, if |x|=|z| then it means |x|=|y|=|z|, thus arg⁡(x)>arg⁡(y) and arg⁡(y)>arg⁡(z), which gives arg⁡(x)>arg⁡(z) and x>z.

                      Ex 1.37

                      Solution

                      Axiom 6 is satisfied. Axiom 7 is satisfied. Axiom 8 is not satisfied since for x=(1,1),y=(1,2), we have xy=(−1,3), then x>0,y>0 but xy<0. Axiom 9 is satisfied.

                      Ex 1.38

                      State and prove a theorem analogous to Theorem 1.48, expressing arg⁡(z1/z2) in terms of arg⁡(z1) and arg⁡(z2).

                      Solution

                      Theorem: If z1/z2≠0, then arg⁡(z1/z2)=arg⁡(z1)−arg⁡(z2)+2πn(z1,z2), where

                      n(z1,z2)={ 0,if −π<arg⁡(z1)−arg⁡(z2)≤+π,+1,if −2π<arg⁡(z1)−arg⁡(z2)≤−π,−1,if π<arg⁡(z1)−arg⁡(z2)<2π.

                      Proof. Write z1=|z1|eiθ1,z2=|z2|eiθ2, where θ1=arg⁡(z1) and θ2=arg⁡(z2). Since −π<θ1≤+π and −π<θ2≤+π, we have −2π<θ1−θ2<2π. Hence there is an integer n such that −π<θ1−θ2+2nπ≤π. this n is the same as the integer n(z1,z2) given in the theorem, and for this n we have arg⁡(z1/z2)=θ1−θ2+2πn.

                      Ex 1.39

                      State and prove a theorem analogous to Theorem 1.54, expressing Log (z1/z2) in terms of Log (z1) and Log (z2).

                      Solution

                      Theorem: If z1/z2≠0, then

                      Log (z1/z2)=Log z1−Log z2+2πin(z1,z2)

                      where n(z1,z2) is the integer defined in theorem in Exercise 1.38. Proof.

                      Log (z1/z2)=log⁡|z1/z2|+iarg⁡(z1/z2)=log⁡|z1|−log⁡|z2|+i[arg⁡(z1)−arg⁡(z2)+2πn(z1,z2)]

                      Ex 1.40

                      Prove that the nth roots of 1 (also called the nth roots of unity) are given by α,α2,…,αn, where α=e2πi/n, and show that the roots ≠1 satisfy the equation

                      1+x+x2+⋯+xn−1=0.

                      Proof

                      Use Theorem 1.51, the nth roots of 1 can be written as zk=Reiϕk,k=0,1,…,n−1, where R=|1|1/n=1 and

                      ϕk=arg⁡(1)n+2πkn=2πkn,(k=0,1,…,n−1)

                      thus zk=(e2πi/n)k=αk with k=0,1,…,n−1. Notice that α0=1 and αn=e2πi=cos⁡2π+isin⁡2π=1, thus z0=αn. Since

                      1+x+x2+⋯+xn−1=1−xn1−x,x≠1

                      the roots of 1 which ≠1 obviously satisfy the equation.
