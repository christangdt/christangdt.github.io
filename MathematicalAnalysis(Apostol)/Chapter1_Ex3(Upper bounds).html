              Upper bounds

              Ex 1.18

              Show that the sup and inf of a set are uniquely determined whenever they exist.

              Solution

              Suppose a set S has two sups α=supS and α′=supS, then by definition of sup, we have α≤α′ and α′≤α, thus α=α′. The case of inf can be similarly proved.

              Ex 1.19

              Find the sup and inf of each of the following sets of real numbers: a) All numbers of the form 2−p+3−q+5−r, where p,q,r take on all positive integer values. b) S={x:3x2−10x+3<0}. c) S={x:(x−a)(x−b)(x−c)(x−d)<0}, where a<b<c<d.

              Solution

              a) the sup is 3 and the inf is 0. b) 3x2−10x+3=(3x−1)(x−3), thus the sup is 3 and the inf is 1/3. c) the sup is d and the inf is a.

              Ex 1.20

              Prove the comparison property for suprema (Theorem 1.16).

              Proof

              Let α=supT, then α≥t,∀t∈T, which means α is an upper bound of S, by the completeness Axiom, S has a supremum supS. Assume α<supS, then by Theorem 1.14 ∃s∈S, such that α<s≤supS, since T is nonempty, there is t∈T, then we have t≤α<s, a contradiction to s≤t. Thus we must have supS≤α=supT.

              Ex 1.21

              Let A and B be two sets of positive numbers bounded above, and let a=supA,b=supB. Let C be the set of all products of the form xy, where x∈A and y∈B. Prove that ab=supC.

              Proof

              Obviously a>0,b>0, for ∀c∈C, there is x∈A,y∈B s.t. c=xy≤ab, thus ab is an upper bound of C. Next for ∀ε>0, let δ=min{ε,1/(a+b),a+b}, then by definition of a and b, we can find x′∈A,y′∈B such that

              a−δ2<x′,b−δ2<y′

              let c′=x′y′, then

              c′>(a−δ2)(b−δ2)=ab−[(a+b−δ2)δ]δ≥ab−[(a+b)δ]δ≥ab−ε

              which shows ab is the least upper bound.

              Ex 1.22

              Given x>0 and an integer k≥2. Let a0 denote the largest integer ≤x and, assuming that a0,a1,…,an−1 have been defined, let an denote the largest integer such that

              a0+a1k+a2k2+⋯+ankn≤x

              a) Prove that 0≤ai≤k−1 for each i=1,2,… b) Let rn=a0+a1k−1+a2k−2+⋯+ank−n and show that x is the sup of the set of rational numbers r1,r2,…

              Solution

              a) If ai>k−1, then write ai=k+δ, we have δ≥0 and

              a0+a1k+⋯+ai−1+1ki−1≤a0+a1k+⋯+ai−1+1ki−1+δki=a0+a1k+⋯+aiki≤x

              gives a contradiction to the finding of ai−1. If ai<0, we suppose 0≤aj≤k−1 for each j=1,…,i−1, as ai is the largest integer to make

              a0+a1k+a2k2+⋯+aiki≤x

              we know that

              a0+a1k+a2k2+⋯+ai−1ki−1=a0+a1k+a2k2+⋯+0ki>x

              this contradicts the search of ai−1. b) It is easy to see x≥rn for all rn, thus x is an upper bound of the set {r1,r2,…}, we also have x−rn<1/kn for each n, thus for any x′<x, we choose integer m such that 1/km<x−x′, then x′<x−1/km<rm≤x, which means x′ is not an upper bound of {r1,r2,…}
