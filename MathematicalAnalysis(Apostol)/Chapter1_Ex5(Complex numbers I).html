                    Ex 1.27

                    Express the following complex numbers in the form a+bi. a) (1+i)3 b) (2+3i)/(3−4i) c) i5+i16 d) 12(1+i)(1+i−8).

                    Solution

                    a) (1+i)3=2i(1+i)=−2+2i b) (2+3i)/(3−4i)=(2+3i)(3+4i)/25=(−6+17i)25=−6/25+(17/25)i c) i5+i16=i+(i4)4=1+i d) since i2=−1, we have i4=1 and i8=1, thus 12(1+i)(1+i−8)=1+i.

                    Ex 1.30

                    Describe geometrically the set of complex numbers z which satisfies each of the following conditions: a) |z|=1 b) |z|<1 c) |z|≤1 d) z+z¯=1 e) z−z¯=i f) z¯+z=|z|2

                    Solution

                    On the complex plane a) is points on the unit circle. b) is points inside the unit circle c) is points not outside the unit circle d) the verticle line which cross the point (1/2,0) e) the horizontal line which cross the point (0,1/2) f) Let z=a+bi, then z+z¯=2a and |z|2=a2+b2, and 2a=a2+b2 means (a−1)2+b2=1, thus this represents points on the circle which has center at (1,0) and radius 1.

                    Ex 1.31

                    Given three complex numbers z1,z2,z3 such that |z1|=|z2|=|z3|=1 and z1+z2+z3=0. Show that these numbers are vertices of an equilateral triangle inscribed in the unit circle with center at the origin.

                    Solution

                    We only need to prove |z1−z2|=|z2−z3|=|z1−z3|, by symmetry we only need to prove one equality. Define zk=ak+bki,k=1,2,3, then ak2+bk2=1 for k=1,2,3, thus

                    |z1−z2|2=(a1−a2)2+(b1−b2)2=2−2(a1a2+b1b2)|z1−z2|2=|2z1+z3|2=(2a1+a3)2+(2b1+b3)2=5+4(a1a3+b1b3)

                    which gives 2(a1a2+b1b2)+4(a1a3+b1b3)+3=0 Similarly, we have

                    |z1−z3|2=2−2(a1a3+b1b3)|z1−z3|2=|2z1+z2|2=5+4(a1a2+b1b2)

                    which gives 2(a1a3+b1b3)+4(a1a2+b1b2)+3=0 From (1)(2) we have (a1a2+b1b2)=(a1a3+b1b3)=−1/2 and

                    |z1−z2|2=|z1−z3|2=3

                    Ex 1.32

                    If a and b are complex numbers, prove that: a) |a−b|2≤(1+|a|2)(1+|b|2) b) If a≠0, then |a+b|=|a|+|b| if and only if b/a is real and nonnegative.

                    Proof

                    a) We have

                    (1+|a|2)(1+|b|2)−|a−b|2=(1+aa¯)(1+bb¯)−(a−b)(a¯−b¯)=1+aa¯+bb¯+aba¯b¯−aa¯−bb¯+ab¯+ba¯=1+ab¯+ba¯+aba¯b¯=(1+ab¯)(1+a¯b)=(1+ab¯)2≥0

                    b) a≠0 means |a|≠0, thus

                    |a+b||a|=|a+ba|=|1+ba|,|a|+|b||a|=1+|b||a|=1+|ba|

                    Now one direction is obvious. For another direction, if |a+b|=|a|+|b|, as b/a is a complex number, we let b/a=x+iy, then |1+x+iy|=1+|x+iy|, or

                    (1+x)2+y2=1+x2+y2

                    which gives

                    (1+x)2+y2=1+x2+y2+2x2+y2⇒2x=2x2+y2

                    thus x is nonnegative, also 2x=2x2+y2 means 4x2=4x2+4y2, thus y=0 and the proof is complete.

                    Ex 1.33

                    If a and b are complex numbers, prove that

                    |a−b|=|1−a¯b|

                    if and only if |a|=1 or |b|=1. For which a and b is the inequality |a−b|<|1−a¯b| valid?

                    Solution

                    Use zz¯=|z|2 we have

                    |a−b|2−|1−ab¯|2=(a−b)(a¯−b¯)−(1−ab¯)(1−a¯b)=|a|2+|b|2−|a|2|b|2−1=(|a|2−1)(|b|2−1)

                    so |a−b|=|1−a¯b| if and only if |a|=1 or |b|=1. If |a−b|<|1−a¯b|, then (|a|2−1)(|b|2−1)<0, thus |a|<1,|b|>1 or |a|>1,|b|<1.
