                  Ex 1.44

                  i) If θ and a are real numbers, −π<θ≤+π, prove that

                  (cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)a=cos⁡(aθ)+isin⁡(aθ).

                  ii) Show that, in general, the restriction −π<θ≤+π is necessary in (i) by taking θ=−π,a=1/2.

                  iii) If a is an integer, show that the formula in (i) holds without any restriction on θ. In this case it is known as DeMoivre's theorem.

                  Solution

                  i) cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ=eiθ, thus

                  (cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)a=(eiθ)a=eiaθ=cos⁡(aθ)+isin⁡(aθ)

                  ii) when θ=−π,a=1/2, we have (cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)a=(−1)1/2, and cos⁡(aθ)+isin⁡(aθ)=−i. iii) Let θ′=θ+2kπ, with k being the appropriate integer such that −π<θ′≤π, then by (i) we have

                  (cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)a=(cos⁡θ′+isin⁡θ′)a=cos⁡(aθ′)+isin⁡(aθ′)

                  notice aθ′=a(θ+2kπ)=aθ+2kaπ, thus

                  cos⁡(aθ′)=cos⁡(aθ),sin⁡(aθ′)=sin⁡(aθ)

                  Ex 1.45

                  Use DeMoivre's theorem to derive the trigonometric identities

                  sin⁡3θ=3cos2⁡θsin⁡θ−sin3⁡θcos⁡3θ=cos3⁡θ−3cos⁡θsin2⁡θ

                  valid for real θ. Are these valid when θ is complex?

                  Solution

                  DeMoivre's theorem tells (cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)3=cos⁡(3θ)+isin⁡(3θ), which gives

                  cos3⁡θ+3icos2⁡θsin⁡θ−3cos⁡θsin2⁡θ−isin3⁡θ=cos⁡(3θ)+isin⁡(3θ)

                  Since θ is real, both sin⁡θ and cos⁡θ is real, thus the equation equals to

                  (cos3⁡θ−3cos⁡θsin2⁡θ,3cos2⁡θsin⁡θ−sin3⁡θ)=(cos⁡(3θ),sin⁡(3θ))

                  and the conclusion holds. When θ is complex, we use Definition 1.58 to get

                  cos⁡(3θ)=e3iθ+e−3iθ2,sin⁡(3θ)=e3iθ−e−3iθ2i

                  and

                  cos3⁡θ−3cos⁡θsin2⁡θ=(eiθ+e−iθ2)3−3(eiθ+e−iθ)(eiθ−e−iθ)28i2=eiθ+e−iθ8((eiθ+e−iθ)2+3(eiθ−e−iθ)2)=eiθ+e−iθ8(4e2iθ+4e−2iθ−4)=(eiθ+e−iθ)(e2iθ+e−2iθ−1)2=e3iθ+e−3iθ2=cos⁡(3θ)

                  also we have (write z=iθ)

                  3cos2⁡θsin⁡θ−sin3⁡θ=3(ez+e−z)24ez−e−z2i−(ez−e−z)38i3=ez−e−z8i(3(ez+e−z)2+(ez−e−z)2)=ez−e−z8i(4ez+4e−z+4)=e3z−e−3z2i=sin⁡(3θ)

                  Ex 1.47

                  Let w be a given complex number. If w≠±1, show that there exist two values of z=x+iy satisfying the conditions cos⁡z=w and −π<x≤+π. find these values when w=i and when w=2.

                  Solution

                  Write eiz=a=e−y(cos⁡x+isin⁡x), then cos⁡z=w means a+a−1=2w, or equivalently

                  (a−w)2=w2−1

                  Since w≠±1, w2−1 has two different square roots in the complex field, thus a has two values, so is z. When w=i, solve (a−i)2=−2 we see that a=(1+2)i or a=(1−2)i, thus (x,y)=(π/2,log⁡(2−1)) or (x,y)=(−π/2,log⁡(2+1)). When w=2, solve (a−2)2=3 we see that a=2+3 or a=2−3, thus (x,y)=(0,log⁡(2−3) or (x,y)=(0,log⁡(2+3).

                  Ex 1.49

                  a) By equating imaginary parts in DeMoivre's formula prove that

                  sin⁡nθ=sinn⁡θ{(n1)cotn−1⁡θ−(n3)cotn−3⁡θ+(n5)cotn−5⁡θ−⋯}

                  b) If 0<θ<π/2, prove that

                  sin⁡(2m+1)θ=sin2m+1⁡θPm(cot2⁡θ)

                  where Pm is the polynomial of degree m given by

                  Pm(x)=(2m+11)xm−(2m+13)xm−1+(2m+15)xm−2−⋯

                  Use this to show that Pm has zeros at the m distinct points xk=cot2⁡{πk/(2m+1)} for k=1,2,…,m

                  c) Show that the sum of the zeros of Pm is given by

                  ∑k=1mcot2⁡πk2m+1=m(2m−1)3,

                  and that the sum of their squares is given by

                  ∑k=1mcot4⁡πk2m+1=m(2m−1)(4m2+10m−9)45.

                  Solution

                  a) the DeMoivre's formula tells

                  (cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)n=cos⁡(nθ)+isin⁡(nθ)

                  for the left side, the imaginary part is

                  i(n1)cos⁡θsinn−1⁡θ+i3(n3)cos3⁡θsinn−3⁡θ+i5(n5)cos5⁡θsinn−5⁡θ+⋯

                  which equals to

                  i(n1)cos⁡θsin⁡θsinn⁡θ−i(n3)cos3⁡θsin3⁡θsinn⁡θ+i(n5)cos5⁡θsin5⁡θsinn⁡θ+⋯

                  which proves the result. b) let n=2m+1 in (a), we get

                  sin⁡(2m+1)θ=sin2m+1⁡θ{(2m+11)cot2m+1−1⁡θ−(2m+13)cot2m+1−3⁡θ+(2m+15)cot2m+1−5⁡θ−⋯}=sin2m+1⁡θ{(2m+11)cot2m⁡θ−(2m+13)cot2(m−1)⁡θ+(2m+15)cot2(m−2)⁡θ−⋯}=sin2m+1⁡θPm(cot2⁡θ)

                  and when αk=πk/(2m+1) for k=1,2,…,m, we have 0<αk<π/2 and sin⁡αk>0 for all k=1,2,…,n , so

                  sin2m+1⁡αkPm(cot2⁡αk)=sin⁡(πk)=0⇒Pm(cot2⁡αk)=0

                  c) Denote the zero points of Pm(x) with x1,…,xm, then

                  Pm(x)=a(x−x1)⋯(x−xm),a=(2m+11)

                  by comparing the coefficient of xm−1, we have

                  −a∑k=1mxk=−(2m+13)⇒∑k=1mxk=(2m+13)(2m+11)=(2m)!3!(2m−2)!=m(2m−1)3

                  and the fact that the coefficient of xm−2 in Pm(x)/a is ∑1≤i<j≤nxixj, we have

                  ∑k=1nxk2=(m(2m−1)3)2−2(2m+15)a=m2(2m−1)29−2(2m)!5!(2m−4)!=m2(2m−1)29−(m)(2m−1)(m−1)(2m−3)15=m(2m−1)45(5m(2m−1)−3(m−1)(2m−3))=m(2m−1)45(10m2−5m−(6m2−15m+9))=m(2m−1)45(4m2+10m−9)

                  Ex 1.50

                  zn−1=∏k=0n−1(z−e2πik/n)=∏k=1n(z−e2πik/n)

                  From this equality we can get

                  zn−1+⋯+1=∏k=1n−1(z−e2πik/n)

                  let z=1 we can get the formula.
