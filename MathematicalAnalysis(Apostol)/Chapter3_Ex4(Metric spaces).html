                            Exercise 3.26

                            In any metric space (M,d), prove that the empty set ∅ and the whole space M are both open and closed.

                            Proof

                            The empty set is open. For the assersion that all points in ∅ are interior points, we have no x∈M such that x∈∅, thus this assersion is true. Thus the whole space M is closed. The whole space M is open, since for all x∈M and any ball B(x), we shall have B(x)⊆M, thus x is an interior point of M. This means ∅ is closed.

                            Exercise 3.31

                            In a metric space (M,d) the closed ball of radius r>0 about a point a in M is the set B―(a;r)={x:d(x,a)≤r}. a) Prove that B―(a;r) is a closed set. b) Give an example of a metric space in which B―(a;r) is not the closure of the open ball B(a;r).

                            Proof

                            a) If x is an adherent point of B―(a;r), assume d(a,x)>r, then let r′:=(d(a,x)−r)/2 and the ball B(x,r′)∩B―(a;r)=∅, a contradiction to x being an adherent point of B―(a;r), thus d(a,x)≤r, or x∈B―(a;r). It follows that B―(a;r) contains all its adherent points. b) In any discrete metric space with more than two points including a, the open ball B(a,1) contains only a, since no point x≠a can be an accumulation point of B(a,1), the closure of B(a,1) is B(a,1) itself, but B―(a,1) contains all points of the metric space.

                            Refer to Exercise 3.32. Prove that the Lindelof covering theorem is valid in any separable metric space.

                            Proof

                            Let (M,d) be a separable metric space, then there is a countable subset A which is dense in M. Let G={B(a,r):a∈A,r∈Q}, then G is a countable set. Suppose there is an open covering F of M, then for each x∈M, there is an open set S∈F such that x∈S, which means ∃B(x,r)⊂S, since A is dense in M, x∈A―, thus the ball B(x,r/2) contains some point a∈A, choose a rational q such that d(x,a)<q<r/2, then x∈B(a,q)⊂B(x,r)⊂S, and B(a,q)∈G. Since G is countable, we can choose one S∈F for each B(a,q)∈G, the collection S selected is countable and covers M.

                            Given two metric spaces (S1,d1) and (S2,d2), a metric ρ for the Cartesian product S1×S2 can be constructed from d1 and d2 in many ways. For example, if x=(x1,x2) and y=(y1,y2) are in S1×S2, let ρ(x,y)=d1(x1,y1)+d2(x2,y2). Prove that ρ is a metric for S1×S2 and construct further examples.

                            Proof

                            First, obviously ρ(x,y)≥0 and ρ(x,x)=d1(x1,x1)+d2(x2,x2)=0+0=0. If x≠y, then either x1≠y1 or x2≠y2 or both, thus we have either d1(x1,y1)>0 or d2(x2,y2)>0 or both, which gives ρ(x,y)>0.

                            Second, from d1(x1,y1)=d1(y1,x1) and d2(x2,y2)=d2(y2,x2) we have ρ(x,y)=ρ(y,x).

                            Third, let z=(z1,z2), we have

                            ρ(x,y)=d1(x1,y1)+d2(x2,y2)≤d1(x1,z1)+d1(z1,y1)+d2(x2,z2)+d2(z2,y2)=ρ(x,z)+ρ(z,y)

                            This shows ρ is a metric for S1×S2.
